Creation & World War II

Branch State Normal School of California

In 1868 the University of California was established at Berkeley and the Regents of the University of California was established as the governing body. As Los Angeles established a public school system, residents campaigned to create an institution of higher education in Los Angeles as well to serve the growing population. In 1881, California Governor George C. Perkins granted that by signing California Senate Bill 187 into law, creating the Branch State Normal School of California in Los Angeles. The school opened in 1882 in Downtown Los Angeles before moving to East Hollywood in 1914 under the name Los Angeles State Normal School. As Los Angeles continued to grow in size, residents began advocating for a local branch of the University of California. After much debate, the university president agreed and in May 1919 Governor William D. Stephens signed Assembly Bill 626, creating the Southern Branch of the University of California. 

The Los Angeles State Normal School was transformed into the Southern Branch of the University of California and officially opened in September 1919 with over 1500 students. Originally, the Southern Branch offered only a two-year undergraduate program and as a result those who wanted to pursue a bachelor’s degree had to transfer to UC Berkeley or another college. After a strong campaign from the faculty and student body, in 1924 the UC Regents approved the Southern Branch as a four-year degree-granting institution and in 1925 the Southern Branch awarded the first ever Bachelor of Arts degree to 100 women and 24 men. 

Relocation to Westwood

As the university grew, it relocated from East Hollywood in 1927 to the “Beverly Site”, where UCLA is located today. The land was originally the homelands of Tongva Native Americans before it was granted by the California governor to a Spanish soldier. Eventually, Harold Janss and his brother, the owners of Janss Investment Company, came into ownership of the land and sold it to the university at a discounted price. The Janss Investment Company went on to develop Westwood Village and the surrounding neighborhoods and to this day Janss Steps is named after these two brothers. In 1926, the University Regents changed the name from the Southern Branch to the University of California at Los Angeles and the student council adopted the nickname “the Bruins”. 

In 1927, construction of the new Westwood campus location began. The new University of California at Los Angeles campus consisted of a courtyard and four buildings - Royce Hall, Powell Library, the Chemistry Building (now Haines Hall), and the Physics and Biology building (now Renee and David Kaplan Hall). These four buildings were designed by architect George W. Kelham of San Francisco. Along with the new campus, fraternity and sorority houses as well as apartments were being built in Westwood. 

In fall of 1929, 5000 students started classes at the Westwood campus.

The Era of Progress (1930’s) 

Despite the Great Depression, the 1930’s were known as the “Era of Progress” for UCLA, as UCLA expanded both the campus and into new fields of study. Once construction of the original buildings finished, construction of new buildings began. Moore Hall, named after the co-founder of the Southern Branch Ernest Caroll Moore,  and Kerckhoff Hall, named after William G. Kerckhoff, were some of the first additions. The summer of 1932 the Olympics came to Los Angeles and some of the events took place in UCLA’s Women’s Gym, now known as Glorya Kaufman Hall. Also in 1932, Albert Einstein gave a lecture about his Theory of Relativity at Royce Hall. In 1933, a Master of Arts degree was approved and by 1935, UCLA formed its first professional school, the College Commerce (now the Anderson School of Management). 

During the Great Depression students had an increased interest in communism, socialism, and isolationism. As a result, several communist groups formed on campus. President Moore was strongly opposed to these political groups, and UCLA banned partisan politics on campus. In 1934, students attempted to arrange for author and socialist Upton Sinclair to come speak on campus. When President Moore found out what was going on, he suspended five students for trying to “destroy the university”. This caused large student demonstrations and negative publicity, resulting in the student leaders being reinstated. 

UCLA’s Independence

Up until this point, Berkeley controlled the power and finances of UCLA and often would refuse to support UCLA initiatives. In 1933, UCLA alumni together with faculty and administration petitioned Berkeley for their own association. They passed two resolutions, known as UCLA’s “Declaration of Indepence”, stating that the board must be recognized as an autonomous body and be given the power to make recommendations affecting the University as a whole. The UCLA Alumni Association gained official independence in 1934, establishing its own officers, council, and constitution. As the 1930’s came to a close, UCLA’s enrollment had become close to 10,000 students. 

UCLA during WWII

In December 1941, the United States entered World War II, dramatically affecting all aspects of life. Many students and faculty enlisted or were drafted to serve in the military. During the war, enrollment declined by nearly half.  In total, more than 6000 members of the UCLA community served in World War II. The effects of the war could be felt on campus- the Army, Navy, and Air Corps programs moved close to campus, fraternity houses became dormitories and Kerckhoff was turned into a mess hall. A Student War Board was formed to coordinate volunteer efforts, students sold war bonds in Royce Quad and organized blood drives. When President Roosevelet signed Executive Order No. 9066, authorizing the mass removal and incarceration of Japanese Americans living on the West Coast, many Japanese American UCLA students' lives were changed as well. In 1942, the annual USC-UCLA football game fell a few days after the one-year anniversary of Pearl Harbor. To honor the date, students made the game a war bond drive, raising over $2 million.

Post-war

Once the war ended, UCLA started a $38 million construction program through which several of UCLA’s graduate schools such as the engineering, nursing, law, and medical schools were developed. In 1944 the School of Engineering was established following the aerospace work that took place at UCLA during the war. In 1947, Governor Warren authorized $1 million for the construction of a School of Law. The School of Law opened in 1949, making it the first public law school in Southern California. Also in 1949, UC Regents authorized the School of Nursing, as a larger program was needed due to nurses leaving military service and coming to UCLA through the G.I. Bill.
