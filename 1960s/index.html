1960's

California Master Plan

The original Master plan for the University of California was approved by the Regents and the State Board of education in 1959. UCLA’s Chancellor at the time, Franklin David Murphy, strived to develop and increase our institution's autonomy. The major features signed into law that were adopted starting in 1960 were as follows:

  • Differentiation of functions among public postsecondary education would be the UC system (University of California), CSU (California State University), and California Community Colleges. This key distinction allowed each UC to obtain exclusive jurisdiction in public higher education for doctoral degrees in law, medicine, dentistry, and veterinary medicine.
  • Establishment of different admission pools and acceptance rates among these three groups of institutions. The UCs from then on would select students among the top 12.5% of the high school graduating class.
  • Reaffirmation of California’s commitment to the principle of tuition-free education to residents of the state. Costs of tuition would be covered, but additional expenses including dormitories and recreational facilities would not be covered.

Student Unrest/Civil Rights Movement

The 1960s were some of the most divisive decades in world history, specifically in the United States. Political turmoil and civil unrest emerged as students were against the US’s involvement in the Vietnam War. Presidents such as John F. Kennedy,  Lyndon B. Johnson, Richard Nixon, and former First Lady Eleanor Roosevelt all gave speeches on our campus. On January 17, 1969, UCLA Students and Black Panther Party members, John Huggins and Bunchy Carter were murdered in Campbell Hall by a rival black power organization called United Slaves. Earlier in the decade, in 1965, Martin Luther King came to UCLA to deliver his “Segregation Must Die” speech to the students and faculty of our institution. He inspired individuals to take action against segregation, to go out to vote and to propel the civil rights movement forward.

Creation of Inverted Fountain (1968)

Chancellor Franklin Murphy played a pivotal role in UCLA’s history. While serving as Chancellor of the college for eight years, he pioneered the creation of the iconic Inverted Fountain— nowadays found in between the psychology and music buildings. Murphy challenged the architectural landscape by tasking Howard Troller and Jere Hazlett to create a modern vision of Yellowstone Park’s natural beauty. Given the wind tunnel in Franz Hall Court, an inverted fountain was necessary in order to avoid water from being splashed on students, faculty and visitors. The fountain was completed in 1968, during Murphy’s last year as chancellor, and has since become an emblem of UCLA’s campus and has been used in multiple campus traditions. Incoming first-year students come to the fountain to become “bruintized” during their summer orientation and fourth-year students jump in the fountain after they are done with their last spring final, celebrating that they are done with their undergraduate college career.

Creation of Internet

The ARPANET, the world’s first electronic computer network, was created at the University of California, Los Angeles in 1969. The program was first deployed by a student programmer, named Charley Kline, who was supervised by Professor Leonard Kleinrock. On October 29, 1969 the first message was sent from UCLA’s computer in Boelter Hall to Douglas Engelbart’s lab at Stanford. The entire message should have been “LOGIN,” but Stanford’s system crashed and thus only the first two letters, “LO,” were sent. UCLA is known as the birthplace of the ARPANET, which is the precursor to the modern internet. The creation of this program was sponsored by the Department of Defense for new technologies and was then expanded to other UC schools and universities.
